Group A · Matchday 1 · Kicks off 11 Jun 2026

A stadium older than most of its visitors is about to wake up.

There is a special kind of silence inside Estadio Azteca on the morning of a big match. On June 11, that reserve of the crowd will break louder than it has in decades. The old concrete cathedral in the south of Mexico City finally gets to open its world to a World Cup again — and the hosts, El Tri, walk out first.

What's on the line

Why this match matters.

For Mexico

Hosting is a chance to make things right.

For Mexico, it’s not just about winning three points. They reached the Round of 16 seven times in a row, but that streak ended in Qatar 2022 — a tough blow for the country.

Now they’ve had four years to use that energy as fuel. Hosting the tournament is a big chance to make things right. If they don’t come out on top of Group A, it will feel like they’ve let their country down.

For South Africa

A long-awaited return to the biggest stage.

For South Africa, the situation is straightforward but still a big deal. Bafana Bafana haven’t won a World Cup match since 1998, and haven’t been to the tournament since they hosted it in 2010.

If they can return and get a good result against the host team, it would be a huge boost for South African football and put them back on the map in a big way.

What's at stake

  • A. Redemption for the hosts. Mexico’s run of seven straight Round-of-16 appearances ended in Qatar; home turf is the chance to answer it.
  • B. A continent’s dream. South Africa’s first World Cup since 2010 — a good result against the hosts would put Bafana back on the map.
  • C. No group of death, no easy game. Mexico, Korea Republic, Czechia and South Africa are so closely matched the difference is almost negligible.
  • D. Matchday 1 leverage. A few points earned on the opening day can turn into a lifeline by the time Matchday 3 rolls around.
Group A isn’t a group of death where one team outclasses the others — it’s a group where every single game takes on extra importance. — Abdullah Mashuk, Match Previews
Five storylines

Before kickoff, five things to know.

01

A long road to redemption for the host team.

It’s been 40 years since El Tri last tasted knockout-round victory — and that win came on this very field. The country is craving a new era, not just nostalgia.

02

Javier Aguirre is back for his third stint.

A seasoned Mexican coach who has led El Tri at a World Cup before. He brings calm and a pragmatic approach to a team that can tip into chaos when emotions run high.

03

Hugo Broos is rebuilding Bafana Bafana.

The Belgian who guided Cameroon to the 2017 AFCON title is rebuilding South Africa with clever set-piece tactics and a chance for young players to shine.

04

Sons of the fathers.

Some Mexican players were little kids when their fathers played in big tournaments and fell short. Now they have a chance to do better — and make their families proud.

05

Altitude as a twelfth man.

Azteca’s high altitude has always been an extra player for Mexico. But South Africa have prepared by training at high elevation — a smart move not many teams make.

Squad breakdown

Inside the dressing rooms.

Mexico
Manager · J. Aguirre · MEX

Recent form

Mexico look stronger than they have in a long time. Aguirre has fixed the defense, settled the midfield strategy and brought in fresh young players; the Concacaf Nations League and recent friendlies have given real reasons for hope without getting ahead of themselves. Watch the fringe too — a fresh crop of attackers out of Liga MX is pushing, with a substitute capable of bringing extra speed late on.

Strengths

  • A creative connection through Orbelín Pineda and Hirving Lozano, with Edson Álvarez shielding from deep so they can play freely
  • Several wide players skilled at taking opponents on one-on-one, especially down the left
  • Goalkeeper depth and a captain in defense who reads the game like a chess opening

Weaknesses

  • Inconsistent finishing — they can control the game and dominate possession yet struggle to score
  • Fullbacks are solid defenders but sometimes too cautious about overlapping when the wingers come inside
  • Squad temperament can wobble under crowd pressure — a curious irony for a team playing at home

South Africa
Manager · H. Broos · BEL

Recent form

Bafana Bafana came third at the last Africa Cup of Nations — a big surprise to many — and were steady rather than spectacular in qualifying. They head into the World Cup hopeful but grounded. Keep an eye on a talented young winger who has caught the attention of European clubs; if Broos trusts his youngsters, a big tournament could make his name.

Strengths

  • A goalkeeper who has grown into a genuine continental name
  • Plenty of speed on the flanks, and no fear of sending many players forward in transition
  • A solid defense that rarely gives up chaotic goals and is especially strong defending its own box — even troubling some of the best teams around

Weaknesses

  • Goal-scoring depth — when the main striker is quiet, alternative routes to goal are limited
  • Keeping control of the ball against really strong midfields
  • Tournament inexperience for several first-time World Cup players

Q&A · 01

“Can Mexico stay patient against a packed middle?”

Mexico will try to control the game by keeping the ball and pushing South Africa back, using the altitude to wear them down. The big question is whether they can stay patient and keep creating chances even when the first clear openings don’t come.

— Abdullah Mashuk

Q&A · 02

“How will South Africa absorb and counter?”

South Africa will look to block the middle and force Mexico wide, trusting their defenders against the quick wingers. They won’t press constantly — they’ll pick their moments to save energy for counters, and when Mexico get close, Broos drops his front three to form a line of five in midfield.

— Abdullah Mashuk

Q&A · 03

“Why do the opening 20 minutes decide so much?”

If Mexico score early, South Africa must chase the game in energy-sapping conditions. But if Bafana withstand the initial pressure and reach halftime level, the altitude can start working in their favor too — fatigue affects both teams.

— Abdullah Mashuk

Historical & fun facts

The crowd

Mexico City fans don’t support their team — they live in conversation with it.

By the 30-minute mark, even the neutral fans at the Azteca will be cheering South Africa on. — Abdullah Mashuk · Match Previews
Final thoughts

The world’s biggest party is about to begin.

It’s not every day that everything in sport comes together this powerfully. The Azteca hosting a World Cup match is a mix of old and new — historic, yet fresh and exciting. Playing in front of their home crowd is familiar for Mexico, but also uncharted: this level of pressure, with the whole world watching, is a big test.
South Africa can just go out and play with nothing to lose. No one thinks they’ll win — and that mindset has produced big opening-day surprises before, as Bafana proved in 2010. Whatever the outcome on June 11, this game will be unforgettable. Lace up the boots, queue up the chants, and pour something cold.

Common questions · Mexico vs South Africa

Things people actually ask us.

When is Mexico vs South Africa? –
Thursday, June 11, 2026. Kickoff is at 12:00 p.m. Eastern Time / 9:00 a.m. Pacific Time.
Where is the match being played? +
Estadio Azteca in Mexico City, Mexico — a newly refurbished ground with a capacity of approximately 87,000.
Where can fans in the U.S. watch the match? +
FOX (English) and Telemundo (Spanish) are expected to carry coverage.
Why does this match open the tournament? +
It is the first match of Group A and the opening game of the 2026 World Cup — the unofficial ribbon-cutting of a 48-team, three-nation summer.
Who else is in Group A? +
Mexico, South Africa, Korea Republic and Czechia — four closely matched teams where every game carries extra weight.
Will the altitude affect the game? +
Likely. Mexico City sits over 2,200 meters above sea level — usually dry and mild, but visitors can start feeling short of air after a while, around the 70-minute mark.
When did these teams last meet at a World Cup? +
In the 2010 opener, also a meeting between Mexico and South Africa — the match remembered for Siphiwe Tshabalala’s thunderbolt. It has been 16 years since they last played.
Which team is favored? +
Mexico, the hosts. Our prediction is a 2–0 Mexico win built on first-half tension and a second-half set-piece breakthrough.
What happens if the match ends in a draw? +
Both teams take a point. In a tightly matched Group A, even a single opening-day point can become a lifeline by Matchday 3.
